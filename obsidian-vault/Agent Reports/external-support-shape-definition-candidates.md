## 1. DNA Double Helix

Internal anchor:
- `D:\AI_Problem_Solver\proprietary_files\physics\Medica_Codexica_Vol_I_.txt`
- Relevant internal concepts: DNA as double helix, helix geometry near 10.5 base pairs per turn, optical/waveguide-like geometry language.

External support papers:

1. Watson, J. D. and Crick, F. H. C. (1953). "Molecular Structure of Nucleic Acids: A Structure for Deoxyribose Nucleic Acid." Nature 171, 737-738. DOI: [10.1038/171737a0](https://doi.org/10.1038/171737a0). Source: [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/171737a0).
   - Geometry support: establishes DNA as a paired helical molecular object with strand topology and base-pairing constraints.

2. Franklin, R. E. and Gosling, R. G. (1953). "Molecular Configuration in Sodium Thymonucleate." Nature 171, 740-741. DOI: [10.1038/171740a0](https://doi.org/10.1038/171740a0). Source: [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/171740a0).
   - Geometry support: provides X-ray diffraction evidence constraining DNA fiber geometry, molecular configuration, and helical interpretation.

Shape-definition note:
- Strong candidate because DNA has stable, parameterized geometry: helix radius, pitch, groove spacing, base-pair normal vectors, and paired-chain complementarity.

Shape-definition note:
- Strong candidate because protein folds can be represented as backbone curves, torsion-angle fields, contact maps, secondary-structure primitives, and surface pockets.

1. Receptor / ligand binding pocket
2. Protein fold / alpha-helix
3. DNA double helix
4. Cell membrane / lipid bilayer
5. Stomatal guard-cell pore

Rationale:
- Binding pockets and protein folds give the strongest immediate path to geometry-first matching because they connect shape, function, and candidate intervention design.
- DNA and membranes provide foundational geometric primitives that can stabilize the ontology.
- Stomatal pores are excellent for cross-domain geometric learning because they add botanical, image-measurable, mechanically modeled geometry.
